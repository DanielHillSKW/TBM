Module 1: Safety Requirements
Before any treatment begins, you must know the safety protocols that protect you, your customer, and the environment.
Learning Objectives
1

Identify required personal protective equipment for fly treatments

2

Understand label compliance and SDS requirements

3

Know how to protect sensitive areas and manage re-entry

Personal Protective Equipment

Nitrile gloves at all times during application

Safety glasses or goggles

Chemical resistant footwear

N95 or higher respirator for aerosols and fogging in enclosed spaces

Label and SDS

Read the full product label before every application

Keep Safety Data Sheets accessible on site

Never exceed labeled rates or apply to unlisted sites

The label is the law

Sensitive Area Prep

Evacuate food prep surfaces before treatment

Cover or remove food, utensils, and aquariums

Notify all occupants prior to treatment

Restrict re-entry until dry or per label

Spills and Disposal

Contain spills immediately with inert material

Refer to SDS for decontamination steps

Dispose of containers per local regulations

Field Note

Always do a pre-treatment walkthrough with your PPE already on. Customers notice and it builds trust immediately. It also sets the tone that this is a professional service, not a casual spray-and-go.

Module 3: Products and Tools
Know your arsenal. Each product category has a specific role. Using the right tool in the right place is what separates a callback from a resolved account.
Learning Objectives
1

Identify product categories and their primary function

2

Match products to the correct application site and target species

3

Understand when to combine product types for a complete program

Pyrethrin Aerosol
Quick knockdown of adult flies. Use in food handling areas per label. Flush applications in cracks and crevices. Example: CB 80, CB PCO Fogger.
Residual Insecticide
Perimeter and resting surface treatments. Crack and crevice at entry points and window frames. Example: Bifen IT, Talstar P.
Drain Gel / Enzyme Treatment
Destroys the organic biofilm inside drains. Apply weekly during active infestations. Example: InVade Bio Drain, Forid.
Fly Bait
Sugar based attractant for house flies and bottle flies. Apply to exterior walls and dumpster areas. Example: Maxforce Fly Spot Bait, QuikStrike.
Insect Light Traps
UV light capture for adult flies indoors. Place away from competing light sources. Change glue boards monthly. Example: Vector Plasma, Paraclipse.
Sticky Monitoring Traps
Monitor small fly population levels near drains and windows. Replace weekly. Example: Catchmaster.
Field Note

Drain gel alone is not enough for a drain fly account. You need to mechanically brush the drain first. Product cannot penetrate a thick biofilm layer without mechanical removal of the bulk debris first.

Knowledge check: You are treating a drain fly account. You apply drain gel but flies persist after two weeks. What was most likely skipped before the gel application?

Module 4: Treatment Methods
Treatment without source elimination is temporary. Work through these scenarios to practice your decision-making process on real account types.
Learning Objectives
1

Apply the inspect, identify, recommend, treat sequence

2

Prioritize source elimination before adulticiding

3

Determine when a third party referral is needed

Scenario A: You arrive at a bar and grill with a complaint of small flies near the bar area for the past 3 weeks. You observe small tan flies hovering around the speed rail and emerging from floor drains. The bar manager says they spray bleach down the drains every night. What is your first action?
Inspect the drains with a flashlight for biofilm and organic buildup before applying anything
Apply pyrethrin aerosol to knock down adult flies and schedule a follow up
Apply drain gel immediately to all floor drains
Recommend the customer increase the frequency of drain cleaning
Correct first step is thorough inspection. Bleach does not destroy biofilm and may actually push breeding further into the pipe. You need to confirm the source, brush the drain mechanically, then apply an enzyme treatment. Adult knockdown without source work will result in a callback.
Scenario B: A dental office reports phorid flies emerging from a specific area of the waiting room floor for 2 weeks. No food sources are present. Flies are running across surfaces erratically. What do you recommend?
Apply residual insecticide to baseboards and treat drains in the restroom
Document findings, treat accessible voids with residual, and recommend the customer contact a plumber to inspect for a possible sewer break under the slab
Place insect light traps throughout the waiting room and return in two weeks
Increase service frequency to weekly and apply aerosol each visit
Phorid flies with no food source in a localized zone is a strong indicator of a broken sewer line or a dead animal under the slab. Treating adults without addressing the source will fail every time. A plumber referral is the professional and correct recommendation. Document it in writing.
Field Note

The fastest way to lose a commercial account is to promise fly control without addressing the source. Set expectations clearly, document what you found, and always put third party referrals in writing on the service report.

Module 5: Customer Communication
What you say to the customer determines whether they trust the process. Use these scripts and practice adapting them to real situations.
Learning Objectives
1

Set realistic expectations at the initial visit

2

Communicate sanitation responsibility without alienating the customer

3

Know what to say when the source is structural or unknown

Initial visit script
"Today we will identify the source of your fly activity and begin treatment. Fly problems almost always involve a breeding source, and until that source is removed, you will continue to see activity. Our treatment will reduce adult populations quickly, but complete resolution depends on eliminating where they are breeding. That may require sanitation changes on your end as well."
Tip: Deliver this before you do anything else. It frames the entire service correctly from the start.
When the source is structural
"We have found evidence that the source may be behind a wall or under the slab. Until that source is physically removed or repaired, fly populations will persist. We strongly recommend contacting a plumber or contractor to investigate. We will continue to treat what we can access, but this situation requires more than pest control alone."
Tip: Always follow this up with a written recommendation on the service report. It protects you and documents the conversation.
Sanitation accountability script
"A large part of what drives fly activity is sanitation and moisture. We can treat the current population, but if drains are not cleaned regularly, garbage is left open, or moist organic material remains present, flies will return. We will document our recommendations in writing. Following through on these conditions will make a significant difference in the outcome."
Tip: Frame this as a partnership, not a criticism. You are coaching them toward a better outcome together.
Setting the follow up timeline
"You can expect to see a reduction in adult fly activity within 24 to 72 hours. However, because of fly life cycles, new adults may emerge for up to two weeks after the breeding source has been eliminated. If you are still seeing heavy activity after two weeks and sanitation improvements have been made, please contact us so we can reassess."
Tip: Giving a specific timeframe prevents panicked calls on day two and builds confidence in your work.
